## Series Context: The Great Sabotage

**Series premise:** The systems around us — cultural, social, institutional — are designed to keep people exactly where they are. This series dissects those systems with the precision of someone who's been inside them, broken free, and documented every mechanism of sabotage.

**Tone:** Not a victim writing about being sabotaged. A strategist who identified the sabotage, reversed it, and now teaches the pattern recognition.

**Parts 1-9:** Already drafted. Do not contradict or repeat core arguments from earlier parts.

**Remaining:** Parts 10, 11, 12 (series finale arc)

- Part 11: The Exit Protocol — How to Leave Systems Without Burning Alive
- Part 12: The Reconstruction — What You Build After You've Survived Everything

## Instructions

### Step 1 — Confirm the Part Number and Theme
If not specified, use the next available part (10).

### Step 2 — Write the Opening Hook
3 sentences max. Must create immediate psychological tension. No soft intros. The reader should feel slightly uncomfortable reading it — like you're describing something they've experienced but never had words for.

### Step 3 — Set the Frame (200-300 words)
What specific sabotage mechanism is this part about? Name it clearly. Give it a framework. Treat it like you're a behavioral scientist who's been tracking this pattern for years.

### Step 4 — The Evidence Layer (400-600 words)
Real examples. Lagos examples where possible. Cultural references. Personal narrative from the 2023 fire year or 2024 void year only when it adds weight, not for sympathy. Reference relevant fictional frameworks (Shelby, Reddington, Scofield) if they sharpen the point.

### Step 5 — The Reversal (300-400 words)
Here's what you do instead. Specific. Actionable. Uncomfortable to read if you're still inside the system. This is where the Metal Dragon framework applies — heat made it stronger, not destroyed it.

### Step 6 — The Close (100-150 words)
Don't summarize. Don't wrap it neatly. End with a question or a declaration that leaves the reader with something to sit with. Substack readers should close their laptop and stare at the ceiling.

## Voice Rules (Non-Negotiable)
- Contractions throughout (don't, can't, won't, you're, it's)
- No hyphens between words
- No em-dashes
- No "Imagine this," "In today's landscape," or corporate vocabulary
- Every paragraph earns its place or gets cut
